Creating an ontology means defining business concepts (entity types), their characteristics (properties), and how they connect (relationships).

Fabric IQ offers two paths for creating ontologies. You can generate initial structure from an existing Power BI semantic model, automatically creating entity types and relationships that you then refine. Or you can build directly from OneLake data, manually defining each business concept with full control over naming. Both paths produce the same result—a complete ontology bound to your data sources.

Power BI semantic models organize data for business intelligence—they define tables, columns, and relationships. For example, a semantic model specifies that `patients` links to `rooms` through `CurrentRoomId`, enabling easy reporting. For ontology generation, the Power BI semantic model must use Direct Lake mode, which queries lakehouse data in place without copying it. This preserves the connection to OneLake's underlying structure that ontology needs. OneLake is Fabric's data lake where your lakehouse tables and eventhouse streams reside.

## Generate from Power BI semantic model

With this approach, you select an existing Direct Lake semantic model and Fabric IQ automatically generates entity types, properties, and relationship types. Each table becomes an entity type, columns become properties, and model relationships become relationship types.

This automation saves significant setup time. Within minutes, you have working ontology structure ready to refine into business vocabulary.

You review and refine the generated structure to match how your business communicates. Autogenerated relationship names like `hospitals_has_departments` are technically accurate but might not match your preferred business vocabulary—you can rename them to something like "hospital contains department." The semantic model provides a strong foundation that you adjust to fit your organization's language.

Choose this approach when you have a well-structured Direct Lake semantic model representing your business domain. Ask yourself: Can you explain what each table represents as a business concept? Do relationships reflect how your business works—like "sales occur at stores" or "patients occupy rooms"? Are most tables and relationships relevant to the vocabulary you want? If yes, even with technical names, the model is a good candidate. You'll invest time customizing names and structure to match your exact business needs.

## Build directly from OneLake

You can also build an ontology directly from OneLake. Instead of generating from a semantic model, you start with an empty ontology and manually create each entity type by binding directly to OneLake sources—lakehouse tables and eventhouse streams. You define business vocabulary from scratch.

You think through each business concept intentionally—which OneLake data sources populate which entities, how properties should be named, how entities connect through relationships. Every element uses business vocabulary from the beginning, defined exactly as you want it.

This path requires more upfront design work than automatically generating from a Power BI semantic model but produces clean structure immediately. No refinement phase, no cleanup of autogenerated technical names.

Choose this approach when you don't have a semantic model, when existing models are optimized for reporting rather than business vocabulary, or when you prefer designing ontology structure from scratch. You need clear understanding of your business domain and how entities relate.

The next unit teaches ontology concepts through manual creation—you'll build entity types with properties and keys, then define relationship types. This hands-on approach reveals how ontology structure works. After that, you'll see how semantic model generation automates much of this process. Both approaches lead to the same destination: a complete ontology ready to connect to your data sources.
