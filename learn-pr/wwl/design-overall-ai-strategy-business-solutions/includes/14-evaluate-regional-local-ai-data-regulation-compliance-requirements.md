## Unit overview

AI workloads deployed across Microsoft platforms must comply with a complex landscape of regional, national, and local regulations governing data protection, AI usage, privacy, and operational risk. Solution architects play a critical role in ensuring that AI solutions align with these requirements before deployment.

This unit equips learners with the skills to:

- Identify regulatory obligations across jurisdictions

- Map requirements to Microsoft AI platform capabilities

- Evaluate compliance risks for AI workloads

- Integrate regulatory considerations into AI CoE governance

This unit draws from Microsoft's Cloud Adoption Framework AI CoE guidance and the Microsoft Learn Generative AI CoE module.

By the end of this unit, learners will be able to:

- Identify key regulatory domains affecting AI workloads

- Evaluate regional and local compliance requirements for data and AI

- Map regulatory requirements to Microsoft governance and platform controls

- Incorporate compliance evaluation into AI CoE processes

- Assess risk and readiness for AI deployment across jurisdictions

## Why regulatory evaluation matters in AI programs

AI workloads introduce unique risks that intersect with:

- Data privacy laws

- AI-specific regulations

- Industry-specific compliance frameworks

- Cross-border data transfer rules

- Responsible AI expectations

Microsoft's AI CoE guidance emphasizes that regulatory alignment is a foundational capability of any enterprise AI program.

### Key drivers

- Protecting sensitive data

- Ensuring lawful data processing

- Avoiding regulatory penalties

- Maintaining customer trust

- Enabling safe and responsible AI adoption

## Regulatory domains solution architects must evaluate

### Data protection & privacy regulations

Examples include:

- CCPA/CPRA (California)

- Colorado Privacy Act

- Brazil LGPD

- India DPDP Act

Key considerations:

- Data minimization

- Consent and lawful basis

- Data subject rights

- Data retention and deletion

- Cross-border data transfer

### AI-specific regulations

Many regions are introducing AI-focused laws.

Examples:

- EU AI Act

- Canada AIDA

- Singapore Model AI Governance Framework

- U.S. Executive Orders and state-level AI rules

Key considerations:

- Risk classification

- Transparency requirements

- Human oversight

- Model evaluation and documentation

- Safety and misuse prevention

### Industry-specific regulations

Depending on the workload:

- HIPAA (healthcare)

- FINRA, SEC, PCIDSS (financial services)

- FERPA (education)

- NIST frameworks (public sector)

Key considerations:

- Data residency

- Auditability

- Logging and traceability

- Access controls

### Local jurisdiction requirements

Cities, states, and provinces may impose:

- Biometric data restrictions

- Automated decision-making rules

- Employee monitoring limitations

- AI transparency requirements

## Microsoft AI CoE responsibilities for regulatory alignment

Based on Microsoft's AI CoE guidance, the CoE must:

#### Establish governance frameworks

- Define enterprise AI standards

- Document compliance guardrails

- Create risk assessment workflows

#### Evaluate AI workloads for regulatory impact

- Classify risk level

- Identify applicable regulations

- Determine data residency needs

- Validate model evaluation requirements

#### Enable cross-functional collaboration

- Legal

- Compliance

- Security

- Data governance

- Business domain teams

#### Provide templates and tools

- Regulatory checklists

- Data protection impact assessments

- Model documentation templates

- Responsible AI evaluation workflows

## Framework for evaluating regional & local requirements

### Step 1 — Identify jurisdictions

Where is the data stored, processed, accessed, or transferred?

### Step 2 — Identify applicable regulations

Map each jurisdiction to:

- Data privacy laws

- AI-specific regulations

- Industry rules

- Local ordinances

### Step 3 — Classify AI workload risk

Based on Microsoft's CoE guidance:

- Low risk (internal productivity)

- Medium risk (customer-facing)

- High risk (automated decisions, regulated industries)

### Step 4 — Assess data requirements

Data requirements should consider the below:

- Residency

- Sovereignty

- Access controls

- Encryption

- Logging

### Step 5 — Evaluate AI-specific obligations

- Transparency

- Human oversight

- Model evaluation

- Bias and fairness testing

- Safety mitigations

### Step 6 — Map to Microsoft controls

Examples:

- Azure regional data boundaries

- Microsoft Purview

- Responsible AI dashboards

- Azure AI Content Safety

- Azure Policy for AI governance

### Step 7 — Document and approve

The AI CoE should maintain:

- Compliance documentation

- Risk assessments

- Approval workflows
