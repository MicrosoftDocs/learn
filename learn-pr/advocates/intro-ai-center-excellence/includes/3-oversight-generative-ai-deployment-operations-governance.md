Infrastructure management is a critical component of adopting any IT solution. Poorly managed infrastructure can lead to inefficiencies, higher costs, and operational risks. If AI infrastructure, model endpoints, and operational controls are over-provisioned or poorly managed, the costs and risks can reduce the financial benefits of adopting AI.

## Specifying AI best practices

When implementing an AI CoE, use it to set the strategy, standards, governance guardrails, reusable patterns, intake and prioritization processes, and skilling that guide AI adoption. The CoE guides deployments through agreed patterns rather than approving every technical change. Platform teams provide governed foundations, while workload and business teams own use cases, domain data, integration, delivery, and business outcomes.

The CoE can define review patterns that help workload teams deploy within clear guardrails. These patterns can include Microsoft Foundry resource and project boundaries, separation between sandbox, development, test, and production environments, and CI/CD quality gates for safety, security, evaluation, and release readiness. The organization should also maintain a central inventory or control plane for models, agents, tools, endpoints, owners, and approved access patterns so teams know what is deployed and who is accountable for it.

## Security

As organizations integrate AI into their workflows, they must account for risks related to data protection, model security, compliance, adversarial attacks, and responsible AI governance. AI-specific threats can include prompt injection, jailbreaks, indirect prompt injection through retrieved content or connected tools, data exfiltration, unsafe content, insecure retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), over-permissive agent tool access, and supply-chain risks from models, packages, prompts, and plugins. Security isn't a standalone function, but an essential component of every AI deployment.

While each AI solution incorporates security in different ways, an AI CoE plays an important role in setting guidelines and supporting teams in secure AI implementation. Rather than replacing dedicated security teams, the CoE needs to collaborate with them to embed security considerations into AI-related processes and align them with organizational security policies. Conceptually, where licensed and enabled for supported workloads, Microsoft Defender for Cloud AI security posture management can help identify AI assets and risks, Azure AI Content Safety Prompt Shields can help detect prompt and document attack patterns, Microsoft Purview Data Security Posture Management (DSPM) for AI can help surface oversharing and prompt or response risks, and the OWASP LLM Top 10 can inform threat modeling.

Keep the scope explicit when recommending these tools. Defender AI security posture capabilities depend on the Defender CSPM plan and supported workloads, such as Azure OpenAI Service, Azure AI Foundry, Azure Machine Learning, Amazon Bedrock, and Google Vertex AI. AI agent posture is in preview; Azure Foundry AI Agents require Defender CSPM, and Copilot Studio agent coverage requires Microsoft Defender for Cloud Apps. Enabling plans, cloud connectors, and agentless protections can require subscription Owner or Security Admin permissions, can affect billing, and can vary by cloud connector permissions and preview availability.

For larger environments that use multiple models, regions, or providers, an AI gateway or API layer can provide a common control point. This pattern can centralize authentication, quotas, rate limits, model and region routing, logging, content safety checks, and resiliency behavior without requiring every workload team to build those controls from scratch.

## Determining governance requirements

Governance is the critical backbone supporting the consistent application of responsible AI principles and practices across an organization. It involves setting company-wide rules, defining processes, and establishing roles for the stakeholders involved in the AI lifecycle.

Responsible AI governance bodies need sufficient financial resources, human capital, and authority to enact meaningful changes across the organization, ensuring responsible AI practices are both actionable and sustainable.

The AI CoE should be an integral part of the governance model, strengthening responsible AI through skilling, development, deployment, monitoring strategies, and data management practices. It shouldn't replace security, data governance, finance, legal, compliance, or workload teams. Instead, it coordinates AI-specific standards and guardrails with those teams.

The CoE plays a critical role in providing AI-specific data standards and best practices while collaborating with specialized teams responsible for broader data governance and architecture. AI systems often handle sensitive and regulated data, so organizations might need to comply with legal and regulatory requirements such as HIPAA, CCPA, or the EU AI Act. They can also use governance frameworks and standards such as the NIST AI Risk Management Framework, the NIST Generative AI Profile, and ISO/IEC 42001. The Cloud Adoption Framework Govern guidance aligns with concepts in the NIST AI Risk Management Framework and can help structure governance practices, but each organization still needs to map controls to its own obligations.

Data governance for AI needs to cover more than source datasets. It should also consider prompts, responses, retrieved context, generated outputs, agent activity, audit logs, data loss prevention, retention, and risks of oversharing sensitive information. Where Microsoft Purview is licensed and enabled for supported AI apps, Purview DSPM for AI can be part of this control model by helping teams understand AI interactions, sensitive data exposure, and policy coverage at a conceptual level.

For Microsoft Foundry, Purview data security for AI interactions must be enabled in the Foundry control plane or through Defender for Cloud, requires a Purview license, and can require pay-as-you-go billing for data security policies; Purview Audit data for Foundry is included with the Purview license. Enabling Foundry integration requires the Foundry Account Owner role, and managing or viewing DSPM for AI requires appropriate Purview or Microsoft Entra permissions, including content viewer roles for prompt and response details. Purview data security policies enforce only for Foundry API calls that use Microsoft Entra ID user-context tokens or explicitly include user context; other authentication patterns might appear in Audit and DSPM Activity Explorer without policy enforcement. Check the current Purview support matrix before assuming specific capabilities such as DLP, sensitivity labels, eDiscovery, retention, network-isolated integration, or Foundry agent and context coverage.

## Cost management

Managing the financial impact of AI systems requires a structured approach to optimize costs while maintaining performance and scalability. AI solutions often involve high computational, token, and storage expenses, making cost control a critical aspect of operational success.

This practice establishes processes and tools to monitor, forecast, and optimize spending, ensuring that resources are efficiently allocated to maximize ROI. AI FinOps controls can include budgets and alerts, monitoring tokens per minute (TPM) and requests per minute (RPM) against quotas, planning provisioned throughput units (PTUs) or commitments where applicable, and using rate limits to prevent uncontrolled usage. A key consideration in cost efficiency is determining whether to build custom AI solutions, use existing models, or route requests to the smallest model that meets the need.

An AI CoE can contribute to cost efficiency by promoting FinOps principles, driving collaboration between finance, engineering, platform, and business teams to ensure that financial considerations are integrated into every stage of AI solution development and deployment. Cost guardrails can include model selection and routing, prompt caching or batching where applicable, GPU right-sizing, deallocating idle compute, evaluation gates before scaling usage, and showback or chargeback reporting so teams understand the cost of their choices.

## Monitoring and optimization

Effective measurement is crucial for selecting, operating, and optimizing AI models. When selecting models from the Microsoft Foundry Models model catalog, public benchmarks and leaderboards can be useful, but they're only one input. Organizations should validate models with representative organizational data and use-case-specific evaluations that reflect the quality, safety, latency, cost, and compliance expectations of the workload.

Monitoring should then focus on runtime observability, not just initial model selection. Useful practices include golden or custom evaluations, tracing requests across prompts, retrieval, tools, and responses, continuous or scheduled evaluations, monitoring for model drift or quality decay, and feedback loops that capture user-reported issues. Alerts, incident response processes, business continuity plans, and model retirement tracking help teams respond when a model, prompt, data source, or integration no longer behaves as expected.

Fairness and responsible AI metrics help identify risks, disparities, and unintended impacts. They don't remove bias by themselves, but they guide mitigations such as data review, model or prompt changes, human review, and additional controls. Organizations should establish a system for continuous monitoring and improvement, using GenAIOps and MLOps practices to set baselines, track progress, manage releases, and identify areas for refinement.
