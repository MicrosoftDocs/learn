Technology is an essential component of teaching, learning, research, and administration. Students rely on digital tools to collaborate with peers, complete assignments, access learning materials, and communicate with teachers. Universities depend on complex digital infrastructures to support advanced research, maintain academic records, and operate large campus environments. K-12 school districts manage fleets of devices used by students of all ages, often across thousands of classrooms or hybrid and remote learning contexts. This rapid digitization has brought substantial benefits, but it also introduced new vulnerabilities that education institutions must confront with urgency and clarity.

![A photo of a K–12 student participating in remote learning with a caregiver.](../../security-management-education\media\remote-learning.png)

Across both K–12 and higher education, cyber threats are increasing in frequency, complexity, and impact. Attackers often view educational institutions as high‑value but under‑resourced targets. Criminals exploit the fact that many schools lack mature security operations centers or unified visibility across their digital environments.

In higher education, universities hold sensitive research data, including federally funded projects, scientific breakthroughs, and intellectual property that attract nation‑state level interest. In K–12 environments, attackers seek personal information about students, families, and staff, viewing it as profitable on the black market or useful for identity theft. These threats can disrupt learning, shut down schools, endanger research integrity, and compromise institutional trust.

At the same time, education IT teams are asked to support more technology with fewer resources. K-12 districts often have small teams responsible for managing tens of thousands of devices distributed across hundreds of schools. Universities must support a blend of faculty laptops, research systems, student devices, classroom computers, and shared lab workstations—each with varying requirements for access, security, and compliance.

Many institutions also operate in mixed environments where Windows devices coexist with Chromebooks, iPads, legacy hardware, and personally owned devices. The challenge is maintaining a stable, safe, and equitable learning environment while simultaneously responding to increasing security pressure.
