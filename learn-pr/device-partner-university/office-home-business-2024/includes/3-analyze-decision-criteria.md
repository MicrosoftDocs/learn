To choose the best Office productivity option, focus on decision criteria rather than individual features. The following five dimensions are helpful ways to evaluate which is the best fit for a given user or organization—they break down some of the most common concerns and priorities to evaluate when choosing a productivity solution.

### Decision criteria at a glance

Use this table for a quick guide to the decision criteria that matter most for your use case.

| **Criterion** | **What to consider** | **Ask yourself** |
| :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **Product model** | How value is delivered over time | Do I want a one-time purchase or a subscription that evolves? |
| **Change tolerance** | How often the experience might change | Do I prefer stability or regular updates? |
| **Service dependency** | Value added through integrated services | Do I want new capabilities that require connectivity, like team collaboration or AI? |
| **AI benefits** | The role of AI in your workflow | Would AI assistance improve how I work? |
| **Deployment style** | Where and how apps are used | Do I want locally installed apps or service-connected access? |

## Product model: purchase vs service

Start with whether you prefer:

- A **one-time purchase** with long-term use of a fixed version, or
- A **subscription service** that delivers ongoing updates and new capabilities

This choice affects cost structure, update cadence, and what "value over time" looks like.

## Change tolerance

Some users welcome frequent updates and new features. Others prefer a more static environment where workflows remain consistent.

Ask yourself:

- Do I want my productivity tools to change over time?
- Or do I prefer a consistent experience year over year?

## Service and connection dependency

Service-based Office offerings deliver value through **integrated services that extend beyond desktop applications**, such as cloud connectivity, collaboration features, and AI assistance. They depend on a reliable connection to make all those services available.

Consider:

- How dependent do I want my productivity tools to be on services and network connections?
- Do I prefer tools that stand on their own, or tools that improve over time through services and feature updates?

## AI feature requirements

AI-assisted productivity—such as writing suggestions, summarization, and analysis support—is increasingly available in service-based offerings.

Common ways that AI adds value include:

- Speeding up first drafts
- Summarizing long email threads or documents
- Helping identify patterns in data
- Generating starting points for presentations

For other workflows, AI might be helpful but not essential—or simply not worth prioritizing relative to stability and predictable ownership.

This makes AI availability a key decision point:

- Is AI assistance a **core benefit** you want built into your productivity tools?
- Or are you comfortable **not having AI features** as part of your baseline experience?

## Deployment style

Finally, consider how you want your tools deployed:

- Locally installed desktop applications
- Service-connected experiences that might span devices and environments

These criteria form the framework you'll use in the next units to evaluate real-world situations.
