To steer K-12 institutions through times of disruption, their decision makers must be familiar with the strategic priorities that drive both day-to-day operations and long term vision. These priorities guide technology adoption, curriculum design, professional development, budgeting, and risk management.

## 1. Student success and wellbeing

Student success is multidimensional. It includes academic mastery, social-emotional wellness, digital literacy, and readiness for future learning or careers. To improve outcomes for all students, technology must support accessibility and opportunities for agency. Tools that strengthen engagement, provide adaptive learning experiences, and protect student data all contribute to those outcomes.

For example, digital tools that reduce barriers for multilingual learners or students with disabilities can help close equity gaps. Systems that identify disengagement early can allow educators to intervene before issues escalate. Ultimately, student success requires the coordinated work of leaders, educators, support staff, and families.

## 2. Instructional innovation and educator empowerment

Many educators spend hours outside of their teaching time preparing content, searching for resources, or grading assignments. This workload contributes to burnout and makes it difficult to deliver consistent, high quality lessons.

To address these issues, institutions prioritize technologies that:

- Simplify lesson planning and assessment
- Support differentiated instruction to meet the diverse needs of students
- Promote active learning
- Facilitate collaboration
- Reduce repetitive administrative tasks

Innovation is not simply about adding new tools. It's about enabling educators to teach more effectively and creatively while ensuring they have time to support students personally.

## 3. Data driven, insight informed decision making

Modern K-12 institutions operate in data rich environments. Student information systems, LMS platforms, assessments, attendance records, device usage, and intervention logs all hold valuable insights. However, when these systems do not communicate, leaders may struggle to extract meaning from them.

Institutional priorities to extract more value from data may include:

- Breaking down data silos
- Creating real time or near real time dashboards
- Identifying trends related to equity, engagement, and achievement
- Predicting emerging needs
- Supporting targeted interventions and resource allocation

Effective use of data empowers leaders to make informed decisions. It also fosters transparency and accountability, two key concerns for school systems as they engage with parents and governing bodies. And, ultimately, it means delivering the resources and conditions needed to foster great learning experiences.

## 4. Secure and simplified IT ecosystems

IT teams have a big responsibility in maintaining safe, effective learning environments. They may manage thousands of devices, all while protecting sensitive information, supporting staff and students in their day-to-day technology use, and maintaining compliance with local and national regulations.

Considering all those demands, IT teams' institutional priorities often include:

- Simplifying device deployment and management
- Reducing manual configuration
- Enforcing security policies consistently
- Protecting networks from threats
- Minimizing classroom interruptions caused by technical issues

Tools that streamline management or automate security workflows directly support IT teams in their pursuit of institutional stability and resilience.

These unique sets of priorities can help educational decision makers understand where to focus their energy when evaluating technology solutions. That way, they can make the biggest impact possible with the resources available.
